The Number 666: The Mark of the Beast, the Gold of Solomon, and the Worship of Mammon

1 Kings, Chapter 10

14 Now the weight of gold that came to Solomon in one year was six hundred threescore and six talents of gold.

Foreword

According to the classical principles of Scripture interpretation and the science of biblical hermeneutics, to understand the meaning of a text, answers must first be sought within the Bible itself. This principle states: Scripture interprets Scripture. Therefore, to grasp the meaning of the number 666, we should find where else in the Bible this exact number appears and look at the significance it carries in that text.

Following this rule, we turn to the Old Testament, and our very first finding mentioning the number 666 is directly connected to the story of King Solomon. It is precisely this biblical context that will serve as the key to understanding.

But before proceeding to the analysis, one important clarification must be made. The 13th chapter of the Book of Revelation mentions two beasts. In our interpretation, we will focus exclusively on the second beast of this chapter, with whose actions this number is associated.

Who is the beast?

Revelation, Chapter 13, verses 15-18

And he had power to give life unto the image of the beast, that the image of the beast should both speak, and cause that as many as would not worship the image of the beast should be killed. And he causeth all, both small and great, rich and poor, free and bond, to receive a mark in their right hand, or in their foreheads: And that no man might buy or sell, save he that had the mark, or the name of the beast, or the number of his name. Here is wisdom. Let him that hath understanding count the number of the beast: for it is the number of a man; and his number is Six hundred threescore and six.

I will share my understanding regarding the text in Revelation 13, to which I have come: it is about worship... Solomon here serves as an example of ultimate wealth and luxury, which was before the eyes of Israel. One could say that 666 relates to the earthly wealth that Solomon gathered. Just as Jesus later repeatedly raised the issue of storing up treasures in heaven.

I would like to clarify the very concept of the "beast" in this context. In biblical prophecies (for example, in Daniel), beasts represent kings or empires—this is stated plainly there—who bring war. In a broader sense, beasts are those forces that destroy nations and incite destructive actions.
In this chapter, there is no direct indication that the beast is specifically a ruler or a man; rather, the beast in Revelation 13 is that which ruins people, a sinful aspiration, that which drives one to sin. This imagery traces back to the ancient serpent of Genesis 3, where it is said that the serpent was more subtil than any beast of the field. The beast and fallen man share the same sinful nature, which is why it is aptly called "the number of a man."

Special attention should be paid to the phrase "for it is the number of a man." Why human, if we are talking about a beast? The fact is that animals are alien to the thirst for enrichment; they do not hoard gold or build financial empires. The beast in Revelation symbolizes the predatory, cruel nature of sin, ready to "kill" for profit. But the object of worship itself (gold, status, economic supremacy) is the work of human hands. Just as the 666 talents were the pinnacle of King Solomon's human wealth, the "number of a man" points to the limit of the carnal, earthly aspirations of a fallen man who has forgotten God.

The beast is the one who teaches sin... and worship... worship of wealth, gold, and Mammon—Jesus spoke of this: "Consider the lilies how they grow: they toil not, they spin not; and yet I say unto you, that Solomon in all his glory was not arrayed like one of these."

The Image of the Beast and the Mark (χάραγμα)

After all, it is not about worshiping the "beast" itself, but the "image of the beast"—everyone molds their own image... of wealth and "success," which they strive for and worship, while being at enmity with others...

"to receive a mark... that no man might buy or sell..." (ἵνα μή — so that no man) — the beast teaches principles (χάραγμα — sounds similar to the word "character," although I do not base the interpretation on this assonance, it seems obvious that the mark of the beast on the forehead and hand represents sinful principles, whereas the seal of God represents God's principles). A mark on the forehead and on the right hand signifies thoughts and actions: a certain image that influences a person's mind, which subsequently affects their deeds.

And the beast teaches that without these principles, you will not have money (the means of buying and selling)—this is the goal and the result of the mark's operation itself (see the linguistic note below). In other words, the one who receives the mark receives the result of the mark—a way of thinking and acting, essentially, a belief and conviction that without deceit he will not be able to buy and sell and will not be rich... in other words, only the one who worships the same image of the beast is a friend... remember the song from the cartoon—"we smashed the cursed competitors to smithereens"—I believe that is how it went... this is exactly about that: "that the image of the beast should both speak, and cause that as many as would not worship the image of the beast should be killed."

Jezebel and Naboth's Vineyard: How the Beast Operates

Here are some deceitful actions that lead to the death of a competitor or a righteous man by the hands of others. A perfect contextual example from the Old Testament is the story of King Ahab, Jezebel (who, by the way, is also mentioned in Revelation as a symbol of wickedness), and Naboth's vineyard. When Ahab wanted to acquire this vineyard, Jezebel instigated worthless men to bear false witness. Naboth was falsely accused, executed, and his property was seized. This is the closest example of how the image of the beast "speaks and acts," killing those who stand in the way of enrichment.

One could say that this mark enslaves, and this is its purpose (see the note below), just as sin enslaves, and here, as already mentioned, we are talking about the love of money, gold, that without which one cannot buy and sell. As it is written, the love of money is the root of all evil.

Counting the Cost: "Here is wisdom"

The words "Here is wisdom," with which the 18th verse begins, meant a call for special attention in biblical times: "And now, pay attention!".

The verb "count" (ψηφισάτω) is used twice in the NT and refers to calculating money: "For which of you, intending to build a tower, sitteth not down first, and counteth (ψηφίζει) the cost".

In other words, "count the name—it is a number, 666 talents of gold," the beast is a metaphor for the lust for gold in this text... gold is sometimes called a "bloody metal"... "Ye cannot serve God and mammon."

An Idol on Earth — Asphalt in Heaven
The Book of Revelation brilliantly brings the theme of wealth full circle. On the one hand, we see the number 666—a symbol of supreme earthly enrichment (Solomon's gold), for the sake of which the image of the beast enslaves and destroys people. On the other hand, at the end of the book, the Heavenly Jerusalem is shown, whose streets are paved with pure gold. The beast teaches to worship money and kill for it, whereas in God's Kingdom, gold is simply a material for the pavement upon which the saints walk. What compels people here to take the "mark of the beast" is placed under the feet of the righteous there.

Linguistic Note from the Nestle-Aland Critical Edition of the New Testament:

It is worth paying attention to the grammatical structure of the text:

«...ἵνα δῶσιν αὐτοῖς χάραγμα ἐπὶ τῆς χειρὸς αὐτῶν τῆς δεξιᾶς ἢ ἐπὶ τὸ μέτωπον αὐτῶν... [καὶ] ἵνα μή τις δύνηται ἀγοράσαι ἢ πωλῆσαι εἰ μὴ ὁ ἔχων τὸ χάραγμα τὸ ὄνομα τοῦ θηρίου ἢ τὸν ἀριθμὸν τοῦ ὀνόματος αὐτοῦ.»

«...and he causeth all, both small and great, rich and poor, free and bond, to receive a mark in their right hand, or in their foreheads: [and] that no man might buy or sell, save he that had the mark, or the name of the beast, or the number of his name.»

The conjunction "and" (καὶ) before ἵνα μή is omitted in the following major historical documents:
* ℵ * (Aleph with an asterisk) — the famous Codex Sinaiticus (4th century). The asterisk means that the word was omitted by the original scribe.
* C — Codex Ephraemi Rescriptus (5th century).
* 1611 — a Greek minuscule manuscript with this number.
* al (from Lat. alii) — and in some other less significant Greek manuscripts.
* vg mss — in some manuscripts of the Latin translation (Vulgate).
* sy, co — in ancient Syriac and Coptic translations.
* Ir lat, Prim — this is how the text was quoted by the Church Fathers: Irenaeus of Lyons and Primasius of Carthage.

The compilers marked this word (καὶ) as possibly absent from the original text.

In favor of its absence is the fact that ἵνα μή is not used with καὶ in other places.

John.6.12: ὡς δὲ ἐνεπλήσθησαν λέγει τοῖς μαθηταῖς αὐτοῦ· Συναγάγετε τὰ περισσεύσαντα κλάσματα, ἵνα μή τι ἀπόληται.
When they were filled, he said unto his disciples, Gather up the fragments that remain, that nothing be lost.

1Cor.1.14: εὐχαριστῶ ὅτι οὐδένα ὑμῶν ἐβάπτισα εἰ μὴ Κρίσπον καὶ Γάϊον,
I thank God that I baptized none of you, but Crispus and Gaius;
1Cor.1.15: ἵνα μή τις εἴπῃ ὅτι εἰς τὸ ἐμὸν ὄνομα ἐβαπτίσθητε·
Lest any should say that I had baptized in mine own name.

1Cor.9.12: Ἀλλ’ οὐκ ἐχρησάμεθα τῇ ἐξουσίᾳ ταύτῃ, ἀλλὰ πάντα στέγομεν ἵνα μή τινα ἐγκοπὴν δῶμεν τῷ εὐαγγελίῳ τοῦ Χριστοῦ.
Nevertheless we have not used this power; but suffer all things, lest we should hinder the gospel of Christ.

Eph.2.9: οὐκ ἐξ ἔργων, ἵνα μή τις καυχήσηται.
Not of works, lest any man should boast.

As can be seen in all examples, ἵνα μή (so that not / lest) stands between an action and the purpose of that action, indicating the goal.

Literal, word-for-word translation of the interlinear:

«...that they should give them a mark on their right hand or on their forehead, so that no one could buy or sell, if he does not have the mark: the name of the beast or the number of his name.»

The absence of the conjunction "and" grammatically links these two parts into a single cause and effect. That is, the prohibition on trade is not some separate, second action of the beast, but the direct goal and result of receiving the mark itself. The mark is applied precisely so that a person finds themselves in a system of fear and dependency.

Let's think about this logically a little more:
Could Solomon have collected exactly 666 talents of gold year after year—which, mind you, is about 25 tons? Taxes are fickle: today they collect more, tomorrow they collect less; such pharmaceutical precision simply doesn't exist. But the Bible records precisely this figure, and therein lies the answer. In fact, 666 here isn't a strict accounting statement. In ancient times, relying on the Babylonian sexagesimal system, this number was used as a phraseological unit. It meant "an incredibly vast, incalculable quantity." And here a profound spiritual meaning is revealed: such an "infinite" number perfectly reflects human insatiability. It is that bottomless thirst for enrichment, the race for gold, in which man will never have enough.

The Testimony of Irenaeus of Lyons:
Historically, there was a discussion around the text of Revelation related to another ancient reading—the number 616, which is indeed found in some early manuscripts. However, as early as the 2nd century (around 180 AD), one of the prominent Church Fathers, Irenaeus of Lyons, in his work "Against Heresies" directly addressed this problem and categorically rejected the variant 616 as a banal scribal error. He asserted that the genuine number is precisely 666, justifying this by the fact that it is found in all the "most approved and ancient copies," and its authenticity is confirmed by witnesses who personally knew the Apostle John. This important historical testimony shows that the early Church was not only aware of the arising textual corruptions but also consciously defended the variant 666 as the only correct one initially embedded in the original revelation.

The exact reference to this historical testimony is the main work of Irenaeus of Lyons "Against Heresies" (original title Adversus haereses), written around 180–190 AD.

Coordinates in the text:
Book V, chapter 30, paragraph 1 (or abbreviated: V.30.1).

In this chapter, Irenaeus of Lyons directly discusses the problem of variant readings of this number and writes the following:

«Such, then, being the state of the case, and this number [666] being found in all the most approved and ancient copies, and those men who saw John face to face bearing their testimony, and reason also leading us to conclude that the number of the name of the beast... must contain six hundred and sixty-six... I do not know how it is that some have erred following the ordinary mode of speech, and have vitiated the middle number in the name, deducting the amount of fifty from it, so that instead of six decads they will have it that there is but one [i.e., 616 instead of 666] ».
(Quote from the classical English translation: Irenaeus. Five Books Against Heresies. Book 5, ch. 30, §1).

One More Coincidence:
It is worth adding that there is one more place in the Old Testament where this number is found. 666 is mentioned in the counting of the children of Adonikam (Ezra 2:13) who returned from captivity. Perhaps there, too, it symbolizes reliance on human strength. And reliance on the flesh and strength is akin to reliance on wealth. Though it is more likely just a random coincidence, one way or another—it is also simply a number.

Conclusion
Ultimately, the mark of the beast is not a physical stamp that will be applied only at the very end of times; it is a condition of the human heart. Someone serves God and receives the seal of the Holy Spirit on their forehead (the mind of Christ and God's principles), while someone else serves Mammon, taking the mark of the beast—the ideology of deceit, profiteering, and power at any cost.
Someone serves God, and someone serves Mammon. By the grace of Christ, we can be transformed from the old carnal man, prone to the mark of the beast, into a new spiritual man in the image of Christ.
